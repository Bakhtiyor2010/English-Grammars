Time Expressions

  • now — I am reading a book now.
  • right now — She is watching TV right now.
  • at the moment — He is cooking dinner at the moment.
  • currently — I am currently learning Spanish.
  • just now — I saw him leave the house just now.
  • today — We have a meeting today.
  • tonight — I will call you tonight.
  • this week — We are planning a trip this week.
  • this month — She is taking piano lessons this month.
  • this year — I am starting a new course this year.
  • these days — People are working from home these days.
  • nowadays — Nowadays, many students prefer online classes.
  • for now — We will stay here for now.
  • at present — At present, I don’t have any free time.
  • still — He is still sleeping.

Uses & Examples

Actions Happening Now

  • I am studying English right now.
  • She is talking on the phone.
  • They are playing outside.
  • We are watching a movie.
  • He is eating lunch.

Temporary Situations

  • I am living with my friend this month.
  • She is working at a café until summer.
  • They are staying at a hotel this week.
  • We are using my brother’s laptop today.
  • He is taking a short course this semester.

Annoying Habits (with “always”)

  • He is always losing his keys.
  • She is always talking during class.
  • They are always making noise.
  • You are always forgetting my birthday.
  • My brother is always borrowing my clothes.

Grammar Note:

Present Simple vs Present Continuous with "always"
Sentence Meaning Feeling
He always talks in class. A normal repeated habit or fact Neutral
He is always talking in class. The action happens too often Annoyance / irritation

We use Present Continuous + always to show that something happens very often and the speaker does not like it.

More examples:
  • You are always interrupting me! (This annoys me)
  • She is always complaining. (I’m irritated)
  • He is always forgetting things. (Criticism)

Other adverbs with the same feeling:
  • constantly — She is constantly checking her phone.
  • forever — He is forever asking questions.
  • continually — They are continually arguing.
