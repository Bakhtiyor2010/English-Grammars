Relative Clauses

Relative Pronouns

Usage:

We use relative pronouns in relative clauses. We use relative clauses to give more information about something, without having to start a new sentence.

Example:

That man over there is called Bill Gates. He started Microsoft.

➡ That man over there, who’s called Bill Gates, started Microsoft.


Use Example
who – for people What’s the name of the man who created the Internet?
which – for things and animals The experiment which worked was the last one.
where – for places This is the town where Albert Einstein was born.
whose – to show possession That’s the man whose sister discovered a new planet.

who (people)

  • The student who studies hard will succeed.
  • I met a woman who speaks five languages.
  • The teacher who helped me is very kind.
  • The man who fixed my laptop is an engineer.
  • She is the girl who won the competition.

which (things/animals)

  • The book which I bought is interesting.
  • The car which he drives is electric.
  • The phone which I use is very fast.
  • The movie which we watched was amazing.
  • The dog which barked loudly scared us.

where (places)

  • This is the city where I was born.
  • The café where we met is closed.
  • The school where she studies is modern.
  • The house where they live is beautiful.
  • The park where we played is nearby.

whose (possession)

  • The boy whose father is a doctor is my friend.
  • I met a writer whose book became famous.
  • The company whose products are popular is expanding.
  • The woman whose car was stolen called the police.
  • The student whose project won received a prize.

Watch Out

✔ We can use who for animals when we give them a personality.
Our dog, who’s called Benji, is eight years old.
When there is a relative pronoun, remember not to repeat the subject/object.
✘ What’s the name of the man who he created the World Wide Web?
✘ This is the experiment which I’m doing it at the moment.

Non-defining Relative Clauses

Usage:

Non-defining relative clauses give extra information. The sentence makes sense without the relative clause. That man over there, who is called Bill Gates, started Microsoft.

Example:

If we remove the relative clause, the sentence still makes sense:

That man over there started Microsoft.

Example Sentences:

  • My brother, who lives abroad, is visiting us.
  • Paris, where we stayed, was beautiful.
  • This laptop, which I bought last year, works perfectly.
  • Mr. Smith, whose son won the prize, is proud.
  • The new app, which is free, is very useful.

Helpful Hint

We use commas with non-defining relative clauses.
✔ Carl, whose sister is famous, is a friend of mine.

Defining Relative Clauses

Usage:

Defining relative clauses give very important information. If we remove a defining relative clause, the sentence doesn’t make sense.

Imagine that there are lots of people in a room. Only one of them is wearing a blue shirt.

The person who is wearing the blue shirt started Microsoft.

If we remove the relative clause, we won’t know which person it is.

✘ The person started Microsoft.

Examples Sentences:

  • The student who studies hardest will pass.
  • The house that they bought is expensive.
  • The book which you gave me is interesting.
  • The woman whose bag was stolen reported it.
  • The phone that I purchased is powerful.

Helpful Hint

• We don’t use commas with defining relative clauses.
• We can use that instead of who and which.
✔ Did you see the programme about the woman who invented Tippex?
✔ Did you see the programme about the woman that invented Tippex?
